Investment Overview

AMCA is a vertically integrated aerospace parts holding company pursuing a three-engine growth strategy: acquiring profitable niche manufacturers, generating government-backed DLA parts revenue, and scaling a PMA commercial aftermarket business.

The business started with a single subsidiary generating ~$30M in revenue. It now operates five companies across electronic components, hydraulics, and flight-critical hardware — with a 10-year model targeting nearly $2B in run-rate revenue by 2035 and $725M in post-corporate EBITDA.

I
Business Model
What AMCA Does
Three interlocking revenue engines in the aerospace & defense parts market

AMCA is building a modern aerospace component platform by combining a disciplined M&A strategy with a proprietary product business targeting the U.S. military and commercial aviation aftermarkets. The company acquires founder-owned, cash-generative manufacturers of specialty aerospace components — businesses with strong customer relationships, stable revenue, and room for operational improvement under centralized ownership.

In parallel, AMCA's new products division manufactures and sells replacement parts directly to the Defense Logistics Agency (DLA) and through PMA (Parts Manufacturer Approval) certification channels into the commercial aviation market. These two channels have structurally high margins, long product lifecycles, and limited competitive exposure once a part is qualified.

The corporate entity serves as the strategic, financial, and operational spine — centralizing procurement, capital allocation, and business development while leaving subsidiary management intact to run day-to-day operations.

Engine 01
M&A Rollup of Aerospace Manufacturers
01

AMCA acquires profitable, niche aerospace component makers — currently including EMC (connectors/switches), Cal-Draulics (hydraulic components), Electrocube (passive electronics), ACPI (precision components), and Payne (flight hardware). These businesses provide immediate revenue, cash flow, and platform scale.

Engine 02
DLA Government Parts Sales
02

Through the Defense Logistics Agency marketplace, AMCA manufactures and sells qualified replacement parts directly to the U.S. military supply chain. DLA contracts offer predictable, government-backed demand with strong margins (~44% EBITDA). The model projects 300 part launches in 2026, scaling to 18,250 cumulative launches by 2035.

Engine 03
PMA Commercial Aftermarket
03

PMA (Parts Manufacturer Approval) certification grants AMCA the right to manufacture and sell FAA-approved replacement parts for commercial aircraft — competing directly with OEM pricing at significant discounts. PMA revenue is modeled to reach $879M by 2035, becoming the single largest revenue driver in the portfolio.

Track Record
Accomplishments Since Launch
Platform built through acquisition, capital deployment, and new product initiation
M&A & Portfolio Construction
Founding — 2024
AMCA Platform Established
AMCA incorporated as holding company with the initial subsidiary (Payne) providing the foundational cash-flowing base and industry relationships in aerospace hardware.
Feb 2025
EMC Acquired — Electronic Components
First standalone acquisition. EMC manufactures specialized aerospace connectors and switches. FY2025 revenue: ~$3.7M, with ~49% EBITDA margin. Goodwill recorded at ~$3.54M.
Aug 8, 2025
Cal-Draulics (CDI) Acquired
Hydraulic systems manufacturer acquired mid-year. CDI contributed ~$6.3M in FY2025 revenue on a partial-year basis, with strong 40%+ EBITDA margins. Goodwill: ~$2.29M.
Dec 8, 2025
ACPI Acquired — Precision Components
Fourth acquisition closed in December 2025 with estimated goodwill of ~$4.24M (formal 805 purchase price allocation pending). ACPI contributed ~$3.7M in FY2025 revenue.
Ongoing
Electrocube — Passive Electronics
Largest existing subsidiary at ~$10M FY2025 revenue. Electrocube manufactures capacitors, RC networks, transformers, and filters for aerospace and defense OEMs with deep historical customer relationships going back to 2019.
Operations & Financial Performance
FY2023 → FY2024
Revenue Grew 15% YoY to $26.4M
Consolidated subsidiary revenues grew from $23.0M to $26.4M between FY2023 and FY2024, with Adj. EBITDA margin expanding from 15% to 25% — demonstrating operational leverage as the platform scaled.
FY2024 → FY2025
Gross Margin Expanded to 58%
Despite a revenue headwind in FY2025 (~10% YoY decline to $23.7M driven by Payne softness and integration activity), gross margins improved to 58.4% — the highest on record — indicating improving product mix and pricing discipline.
2025
SG&A Investment in Corporate Infrastructure
Corporate SG&A increased to $11.5M in FY2025 (from $10.4M in FY2024) as AMCA built the centralized management, finance, and BD functions needed to support the M&A rollup and new products programs at scale.
Dec 2025
$53.8M Cash Position Post-Raise
Consolidated PF cash balance reached ~$53.8M at December 2025, providing runway for continued acquisitions and the new products launch. Cash was primarily held at the Amca parent entity (~$52.7M).
FY2025
Intercompany Consolidation Underway
Full trial balance consolidation across all five entities completed in FY2025, including IC payable reconciliation and uniform financial reporting across QBO/ERP systems — a key operational foundation for the next phase of growth.
New Products Program (DLA + PMA)
2025
New Products Division Established
AMCA launched its proprietary parts program, targeting both the DLA military supply chain and FAA PMA commercial aftermarket channels. Initial CapEx of $5M committed in 2026 to build the manufacturing and certification infrastructure.
2026 Target
300 DLA Part Launches
The model targets 300 successful DLA product launches in 2026, generating ~$9.5M in first-year revenue at ~24% EBITDA margins. These are replacement parts for existing military hardware with known demand signals from the DLA catalog.
2026 Target
PMA Program Initiated
PMA certification filings commenced. Initial PMA revenue projected at ~$69K in 2026, reflecting the FAA approval lag inherent in the certification process. PMA economics are expected to ramp sharply beginning 2029 as certifications accumulate.
2027–2029
Projected New Products Inflection
DLA revenue projected to reach $128M by 2028 as cumulative product launches hit 3,500. PMA revenue inflects in 2029 (from $1.5M to $20M in a single year), reflecting the S-curve dynamics of commercial aviation parts qualification.
Note
New Production Upside Not Modeled
Per the model's own disclosures: new product figures reflect only the military and commercial aftermarket opportunity. Upside from new production platform placements — where AMCA parts are specified on new aircraft programs — is explicitly excluded from projections.

Key Assumptions
  • ▢
    M&A Pipeline ~$640M+ of additional acquired revenue assumed by 2035. Requires consistent deal flow, favorable multiples, and successful integration at scale.
  • ▢
    DLA Launches 300 launches in 2026 scaling to 2,250 annually by 2031. Historical DLA approval rates and internal manufacturing capacity are key proof points.
  • ▢
    PMA Certifications Revenue inflects sharply in 2029 based on FAA approval accumulation. Regulatory timing is the primary variable and is inherently difficult to forecast.
  • ▢
    Corporate Overhead Grows from $2.3M (2025) to $108.5M (2035). If revenue underperforms, this fixed cost base creates meaningful downside on EBITDA.
  • ▢
    New Production Upside Explicitly excluded from model. Platform placements on new aircraft programs could materially accelerate both DLA and PMA revenue beyond projections.
IV
Comparable Analysis & Valuation
Underwriting
Public comps benchmarking · EV/Revenue & EV/EBITDA sensitivity matrices · Implied market cap at exit

AMCA's closest public analogues are the aerospace aftermarket and defense parts rollup platforms: TransDigm (TDG), the dominant acquirer of proprietary aerospace components; HEICO (HEI), the leading PMA parts provider with a 50-year track record; and Loar Holdings (LOAR), the newest high-growth niche A&D components rollup at a comparable early stage.

These companies trade at premium multiples precisely because of the characteristics AMCA is building toward: proprietary parts with long qualification cycles, government-backed demand signals, high switching costs, and recurring aftermarket revenue. The comps analysis below applies a range of EV/Revenue and EV/EBITDA multiples to AMCA's projected financials at three time horizons to derive implied market capitalizations at exit.

Note: Implied market cap approximates enterprise value here, as AMCA's debt load is assumed minimal relative to equity value at scale. Investors should adjust for net debt and minority interests at time of exit.

Why These Comps Trade at Premium Multiples
  • ✈
    PMA / DLA qualification moatsFAA and DLA approvals take 12–36 months and create near-permanent competitive barriers. Once qualified, parts face minimal price competition and enjoy lifecycle revenue.
  • 🔁
    Recurring aftermarket demandAircraft have 25–30 year service lives. Every approved part generates replacement demand on a predictable cadence regardless of new aircraft build rates.
  • 📈
    M&A compoundingTransDigm, HEICO, and Loar all re-rate upward as their acquisition engines prove repeatable. The market pays for the system, not just the current portfolio.
  • 🛡
    Defense spending visibilityDLA procurement is budget-line driven with multi-year planning horizons, providing revenue visibility well beyond typical commercial contracts.
